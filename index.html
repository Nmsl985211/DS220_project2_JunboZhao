# 2. Basic cleaning and preprocessing

The original CSV already has a lot of useful fields, but some of them are strings that I want to convert to more convenient types.

In particular:

1. I convert the `born` and `died` columns to proper datetime values and extract the birth year.
2. I keep the country and city of birth as they are, but I am aware that some country names may be historical.
3. I drop a few obviously empty rows where almost everything is missing.

None of this is very fancy, but I think it makes later analysis a bit easier.
In[5]:
# convert born and died to datetime; errors="coerce" turns bad dates into NaT
nobel["born"] = pd.to_datetime(nobel["born"], errors="coerce")
nobel["died"] = pd.to_datetime(nobel["died"], errors="coerce")

# extract birth year as a separate column
nobel["born_year"] = nobel["born"].dt.year

# very rough cleaning: drop rows where even firstname and surname are missing
nobel_clean = nobel.dropna(subset=["firstname", "surname"])

nobel_clean[["firstname", "surname", "born", "born_year"]].head()

In[]:
Looking back at the eight questions, a few patterns stand out clearly.  
First, the dataset contains a few hundred distinct laureates, and certain countries appear again and again in the birth records, which shows how concentrated the prizes are geographically. The gender distribution is extremely uneven, with male laureates making up almost the entire dataset.
When grouping by decade, the total number of laureates rises steadily over time, which fits the overall expansion of scientific research. Prize categories such as Physics, Chemistry, and Medicine have the largest counts, while others are smaller but still consistent.
For the age analysis, most laureates receive their prize somewhere in mid-life, usually between their 40s and 60s. The average award age changes a little across categories but not by a huge amount. Birth cities also repeat in a similar way as countries, with a few places appearing much more often than the rest.

Overall, the results make the patterns behind the Nobel Prizes much easier to see: concentrated countries, a strong gender imbalance, steady growth over time, and a fairly consistent range of award ages.
